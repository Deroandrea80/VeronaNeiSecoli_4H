PIAZZA DEI SIGNORI

Piazza dei signori

Piazza dei Signori, also known as Piazza Dante, is a square located in the historic center of Verona, adjacent to Piazza delle Erbe. The square originated in the Middle Ages from the development of the Scaliger palaces and has served political, administrative, and representational functions from the outset. Bordered by monumental buildings connected by arches and loggias, the square was born from the development of palaces where political and administrative life was decided, especially during the Scaliger period, and it is surrounded by monumental buildings connected by porticoes and arches, almost creating a sort of inner courtyard. In the center of the square stands the monument to Dante (1865), a 3-meter statue in white Carrara marble, erected during the celebrations of the poet's six hundredth anniversary, who found his first refuge near Cangrande's court after being exiled from Florence.

History of Piazza dei signori

The square was born in the 14th century with the demolition of an ancient neighborhood that extended in front of the churchyard of San Clemente, as a result of urban rearrangements promoted by Ubertino Da Carrara. The opening of the square was intended to give importance to the eastern access tower of the Palace that was being built. Due to its particular conformation, it became a natural stage for tournaments and love contests. According to tradition, it was precisely from the "lords" Carraresi that the square derived its name. The war between the Carraresi princes and the Visconti at the end of the 14th century damaged the square and the buildings so much that it was defined for a period as "of Desolation"; in the 1420s, the Venetians began a recovery project of the space, which became the main backdrop for civic liturgy: for tournaments, jousts, battles, courtships, concerts, and musical festivals, it became "Piazza dei Trionfi" and again "Piazza della Signoria". On July 17th, it was the site of grand celebrations to commemorate the reconquest of Padua by the Dominante in 1509. Following a speech by Father Alessandro Gavazzi, on May 9th, 1848, it became "Piazza Pio IX", the heart of the anti-Austrian popular movements. It then became "Piazza Unità d'Italia" before returning to its original name in the fascist era.

Torre dei Lamberti

Lamberti Tower, with its 84 meters, is today the highest building in the city of Verona. It is open to the public and it is possible to go up to the belfry is Verona tallest building, one of the landmarks of Verona city center. It is located in Erbe square, in the very heart of the old town. Thanks to the modern elevator it is possible to easily get to the top of the tower enjoying a 360° breathtaking view of Verona.

Monument of Dante

In Verona, while staying with Bartolomeo della Scala, brother of Cangrande, Dante found a stimulating intellectual and political environment. As a result of his stay among the elite of the Scaliger family, he produced many works, including the famous "De Monarchia," which analyzes the relationship between imperial and religious power. The lord of the city, Cangrande della Scala, who had been benevolent and welcoming to Dante, received the dedication of the last canticle of the Divine Comedy: "Paradiso." The sculpture is made of second-choice Carrara marble, with a height of three meters, supported by a pedestal. Dante is depicted with his back turned towards Via delle Fogge, his head slightly turned to the left, facing the Palazzo del Podestà, once the residence of the Scaliger family, where he stayed. The statue of Dante represents the first nineteenth-century monument in the Scaliger city. It previously occupied a different position, but specific information about its earlier location is difficult to find in contemporary sources.
